Origins of Falafel Falafel's history is rich and intriguing, with various claims to its origin. Most widely, it is believed to have originated in Egypt, where it was first made with fava beans. Over time, it spread throughout the Middle East and Mediterranean, adapting to local ingredients and flavors. Today, the most common base for falafel is ground chickpeas or a combination of chickpeas and fava beans. The mixture is expertly spiced with herbs and aromatic seasonings, then shaped into small balls or patties before being deep-fried to golden perfection. The Art of Making Falafel: Falafel is not just a dish; it's a labor of love and a showcase of culinary expertise. To create these little flavor-packed bundles, you'll need a mixture of soaked and cooked chickpeas (or chickpeas and fava beans), herbs such as parsley and cilantro, garlic, and an array of spices including cumin, coriander, and paprika. The preparation process is as much an art as a science. The ingredients are combined, and then the mixture is ground to a coarse texture. Some cooks opt for a coarser texture, while others prefer a smoother consistency. These spicy morsels are traditionally deep-fried in oil, which imparts a satisfying crispy exterior while keeping the inside tender and fluffy. This frying process is a crucial step in achieving the characteristic texture and flavor of falafel. However, baked falafel has gained popularity as a healthier alternative.
